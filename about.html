About


An Interactive Public Health & Socioeconomic Data Visualization Platform

The primary purpose of this project is to provide a comprehensive, interactive platform for visualizing public health and socioeconomic data across the United States. By combining data from various reliable sources, it allows researchers, policymakers, and the general public to explore spatial patterns, identify disparities, and understand the complex relationships between demographic factors, health outcomes, and socioeconomic indicators at both state and county levels through comparative views and advanced bivariate analysis.

Data Source

The Digital Mindscapes platform aggregates and visualizes data from two primary, highly reliable national sources to provide a comprehensive view of public health and socio-economic factors across the United States.

PLACES (Population Level Analysis and Community Estimates)

PLACES is a free, interactive web tool that provides chronic disease and other health-related data for small areas across the United States. Originating in 2015 as a partnership between the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), the Robert Wood Johnson Foundation, and the CDC Foundation, it offers high-quality estimates. This data is critical to help state and local health officials, policymakers, and communities identify local health problems, prioritize resources where disparities exist, and develop targeted health initiatives.

ACS (American Community Survey)

The American Community Survey (ACS) is an ongoing, annual demographics survey program conducted by the U.S. Census Bureau. It provides vital information on a yearly basis about the nation and its people, generating data that helps inform how federal funds are distributed. It regularly gathers detailed insights ranging from jobs, occupations, and educational attainment, to income, housing, and structural variables. Public officials, planners, and researchers use this information to assess community needs and provide vital context to public health data.

Data Preparation

The accuracy and reliability of the Digital Mindscapes platform depend on meticulous data preparation. Our data pipeline involves several key steps to ensure seamlessly integration between diverse data sources.

Key Steps in Preparation

  • 1. Data Cleaning: Raw files from sources like CDC PLACES and the US Census ACS are carefully inspected. Missing values (N/A) and erroneous entries are flagged. Where appropriate, missing numerical datasets are handled through logical interpolation or omission based on statistical best practices.
  • 2. Standardization: Datasets from different sources often use varied naming conventions or geographic IDs. FIPS codes (Federal Information Processing Standards) are rigorously verified and formatted to a consistent 5-digit string to serve as the unified geographic key uniting all health and socio-economic variables.
  • 3. Structural Alignment: Variables are mapped out to follow a consistent naming schema within structured dictionaries. This ensures that a metric like "Average Income" aligns perfectly across states and counties.

Data Processing

Transforming gigabytes of raw prepared data into a visually coherent and responsive dashboard requires efficient programmatic data processing.

Key Processing Methodologies

  • 1. Programmatic Aggregation (Python): Utilizing tools like Pandas, large files containing detailed block-level or zip code-level metrics are rolled up to County and State levels. Averages are calculated factoring in population weighting to reflect accurate true representative values.
  • 2. JSON Transformation: The vast tabulated data is ultimately transformed into lightweight JSON object arrays. This ensures the web application loads quickly on the client-side while still holding thousands of data points locally.
  • 3. Regional Groupings: Automated scripts dynamically append geographic identifiers like "Census Region", "Division", and "RUCC Group" onto data objects based on predetermined mapping lists, allowing dynamic filtering out of the box.
  • 4. Dynamic Extremes: Max/Min bounds for gradients and heatmap legends are calculated algorithmically based directly upon the dataset provided, rather than being hardcoded, allowing automated scalability to future variables.

Main Dashboard

The National Perspective

The Main Dashboard serves as the central hub of Digital Mindscapes, offering a big-picture view of the nation's health and socioeconomic landscape.

Significance

By visualizing data at the state level, this view helps identify broad geographic trends, regional differences, and national outliers. It provides the essential context needed to understand how local conditions compare to the broader American landscape. Having a strong national baseline is the first step in recognizing which regions are successfully addressing health challenges and which may require new policy interventions.

What this information conveys:
  • • Location-based Patterns: Instantly recognize clusters of health outcomes or socioeconomic status across different US regions.
  • • Relative Intensity: The dynamic heatmap colors provide an intuitive sense of how states rank against each other for any chosen metric.
  • • Metric Connectivity: Allows for quick mental correlations by toggling through various health and social factors.
Interactive Features:
State Tooltips Hover over any state to reveal precise data values and state-specific details.
Metric Selector Choose from a wide array of indicators, from chronic diseases to educational attainment.
Dynamic Heatmap Watch the map transform as the color gradient adapts to the range of the selected metric.

States Comparison

Comparative Analysis

The States Comparison page enables targeted side-by-side analysis of user-selected states across diverse visualization formats.

Significance

Comparing states directly is crucial for identifying regional successes or challenges. It allows researchers to move beyond the national average and see how specific states within the same region or with similar demographic profiles are performing relative to one another. Sorting and ranking these states helps pinpoint top performers, providing a roadmap for other regions to follow.

What this information conveys:
  • • Regional Benchmarking: Compare states within the same Census region to spot outliers or localized phenomena.
  • • Data Distribution Styles: Use bar charts for rank comparison and treemaps for understanding the relative weights of selections.
  • • Precise Metrics: The side-by-side table view provides the exact numerical values necessary for rigorous reporting.
Visual Guide: Chart Types
Vertical Bar Chart

Best for side-by-side comparisons of state values. This classical view makes it easy to spot which states are above or below the group average.

• Clear ranking of selected states.
• Immediate visual height contrast.
• Includes state labels for easy identification.
Horizontal Bar Chart

Offers a clean, organized list view. This format is particularly useful when comparing many states at once, as it allows for longer labels and easier scrolling.

• High readability for state names.
• Natural top-to-bottom ranking flow.
• Effective for large comparative lists.
Circular Bar Chart

A modern, compact visualization that uses radial length to show value differences. It's excellent for seeing proportional relationships in a visually engaging way.

• Space-efficient design.
• Unique visual style for data summaries.
• Dynamic interactivity and hover effects.
Detailed Data Table

The go-to source for precise numerical analysis. This view lists raw data points and provides powerful options for data portability and organization.

• Exact values for rigorous research.
• Sortable columns and Group by Region functionality.
• Export options: CSV, HTML, and LaTeX formats.
Key Features:
Multi-Chart Views Switch between Horizontal/Vertical Bar Charts and Treemaps to see the data from different angles.
Regional Grouping Quickly group your selections by geographic region for enhanced context.
Interactive Selection Easily add or remove states by clicking directly on the map interface.

Granular Local Insights

The Counties Comparison page provides the highest level of geographic detail, allowing for the analysis of over 3,000 individual U.S. counties.

Significance

Public health and socioeconomic outcomes are often local. By drilling down to the county level, this view exposes differences that state-level averages often mask. Since most public health funding is managed at the county or city level, this data is an indispensable tool for community leaders and local policymakers who need to justify exactly where and why resources should be allocated.

What this information conveys:
  • • Hyper-Local Disparities: Identify specific counties that are struggling or thriving compared to their neighbors.
  • • Statewide Context: View how counties perform within their own state borders using the "Group by State" feature.
  • • Population Density Trends: Understand how urbanization correlates with the selected metric through integrated filtering.
Key Features:
3,000+ Counties Access data for every county in the United States, providing a truly comprehensive dataset.
Advanced Modal Charting For large selections, expand the bar charts to a full-screen view for detailed exploration.
State-County Nesting Dynamically organize your selected counties under their state headers in the table view.

Urban-Rural Comparison

The Urban-Rural Divide

The Urban-Rural Comparison page specifically investigates how population density and geographic isolation influence health and socioeconomic metrics.

Significance

Research consistently shows significant differences between urban and rural health outcomes. This view utilizes the USDA's Rural-Urban Continuum Codes (RUCC) to provide a rigorous framework for analyzing these gaps. Health access can vary significantly in "frontier" rural counties compared to busy metropolitan areas, helping to highlight exactly where rural communities may be underserved and overlooked.

What this information conveys:
  • • Density-Based Trends: Observe if a metric (like poverty or heart disease) follows a gradient from highly metropolitan to completely rural areas.
  • • Localized RUCC Analysis: Filter your custom county selections to isolate and ignore noise from disparate population centers.
  • • Policy Context: Provides empirical data to support the unique public health strategies required for rural vs. urban environments.
Key Features:
RUCC Categorization Counties are automatically grouped into standardized Metro vs. Non-Metro classifications.
Contrast Charts Visualizations are optimized to highlight the contrast between urban and rural datasets.
Focused Map Views Apply filters directly to the interactive map to isolate specific density classes nationwide.

County Distribution

National Benchmarking

The County Distribution page visualizes how every county in the nation spreads across the value spectrum for a given metric.

Significance

Understanding the distribution of data is key to statistical literacy. This view allows you to see the "shape" of the nation's health—is a problem widespread, or is it concentrated in a few extreme outliers? By looking at the "spread" or variance of the data, you can understand if a state has a consistent problem across all counties or just a few highly troubled areas. This provides a powerful benchmark for seeing exactly where a specific county sits relative to the national average.

What this information conveys:
  • • Outlier Identification: Easily spot counties that deviate significantly from the national norm.
  • • National Benchmarking: See the national average line and determine if a region is systematically above or below it.
  • • Cluster Analysis: Observe if certain regions or RUCC classes tend to cluster together on the value scale.
Key Features:
Dot Plot Visualization A clean, interactive dot plot showing the precise placement of every county.
Highlight Filters Instantly highlight counties that are above or below the calculated national average.
Census Region Coloring Color-code dots by region to visually test geographical hypotheses.

State Multi-Metric Comparison

Multivariate Analysis & Correlation

The State Multi-Metric Comparison tool is a powerful engine for discovery, allowing users to move beyond single-variable maps to explore complex interdependencies between up to 12 different metrics.

Significance

True insight comes from understanding how variables interact. Does high unemployment *always* correlate with poor mental health in every region? This tool provides the mathematical and visual evidence to answer such questions. While correlation does not mean one thing causes another, it is the essential first step in uncoverging non-obvious connections and asking better research questions about geographic differences.

Advanced Visualizations:
Bubble Chart (3 Metrics)
Explore three-way relationships by mapping data to the X-axis, Y-axis, and the relative size of each bubble. Perfect for seeing how population scale impacts bivariate correlations.
Radar Chart (8 Metrics)
Create multidimensional "profiles" for selected states. By plotting up to 8 metrics on a radial axis, you can visually compare the unique "shape" of each state's socioeconomic health.
Heatmap Matrix (12 Metrics)
The ultimate correlation tool. Compare up to 12 metrics simultaneously across all selected states in a normalized grid, using color intensity to instantly spot patterns.
What this information conveys:
  • • Inter-Metric Interaction: Visualize if growth in economic factors corresponds directly with improvements in health risk behaviors.
  • • Normalized Benchmarking: The integrated normalization formula ensures that metrics with different units (percentages vs. raw counts) can be compared fairly.
  • • Regional Fingerprinting: Identify if certain geographic regions share a distinct multivariate profile, such as high-cost housing combined with low food insecurity.

Bivariate Analysis

Spatial Correlation Discovery

The Bivariate Analysis page allows for the simultaneous exploration of two variables, identifying geographic hotspots where health and socioeconomic factors intersect.

Significance

Traditional maps show one variable at a time, but problems rarely exist in isolation. This view uses a sophisticated 3x3 color matrix to reveal relationships between pairs of metrics (e.g., "Is poor health insurance access physically concentrated in areas with high unemployment?"). By classifying data into tertiles, we expose the underlying spatial correlation, helping researchers spot High-High or Low-Low clusters that would be invisible on standard maps.

What this information conveys:
  • • Composite Vulnerability: Identify "High-High" areas where multiple risk factors overlap, signaling a need for integrated policy responses.
  • • Hotspot Analysis: Use the 3x3 matrix to instantly see which areas deviate from national norms across two axes of data.
  • • Intersectional Insights: Discover if socioeconomic gains in a county are effectively trailing behind health outcome improvements or vice versa.
Key Features:
3x3 Bivariate Matrix A custom-designed legend that makes interpreting complex geographic correlations intuitive.
Dynamic Tertiles Data-driven classification that automatically adjusts the color scale to the distribution of selected metrics.
Intersection Tooltips Hover over any county to see the exact percentage values for both Metrics X and Y simultaneously.

Correlation Analysis

Statistical Relationship Discovery

The Correlation Analysis page provides an interactive, color-coded grid designed to reveal the statistical strength and direction of relationships between multiple socioeconomic and public health metrics simultaneously.

Significance

While maps show us where issues occur, correlations help us understand how issues might be linked. By calculating Pearson correlation coefficients across thousands of counties, this tool quickly highlights robust systemic patterns—such as the strong negative correlation often found between income levels and chronic disease prevalence.

Key Features:
  • • Customizable Plotting Matrix: Build your own grids by mixing and matching specific rows and columns of data, tailored to your distinct research inquiries.
  • • Multiple Layout Styles: Toggle between traditional numeric squares, visual correlogram circles, or mixed layouts to best represent the density and significance of variables.
  • • Scatterplot Deep-Dive: Clicking any single square within the matrix dynamically generates a detailed 2D scatterplot revealing the exact distribution of counties making up that specific correlation.

Regression Analysis

Multivariate Predictive Modeling (OLS)

The Regression Analysis (OLS Regression Sandbox) enables researchers to move beyond simple correlations to true predictive modeling. Select a dependent variable and multiple independent predictors to build a classic Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) model directly in the browser.

Significance

While bivariate correlation shows if two things move together, multivariate regression reveals if those relationships hold true when controlling for other factors. This is critical for policy makers seeking to understand confounding variables—such as whether a link between poverty and health outcomes persists after accounting for regional urbanicity or healthcare access.

Key Features:
  • • Custom Model Builder: Select one dependent variable (Y) and as many independent predictors (X) as needed from our comprehensive dataset.
  • • Statistical Output: Instantly generates a full results table including Coefficients, Standard Errors, t-values, and p-values for every predictor, alongside R-Squared and F-Statistics.
  • • Diagnostic Visuals: Dynamic Residual vs. Fitted value plots help researchers assess model assumptions and identify potential outliers or heteroscedasticity.

Dimensionality Reduction

Multi-Method Dimensionality Reduction

The Dimensionality Reduction page empowers researchers to condense high-dimensional health datasets into interpretable 2D visualizations using both linear and non-linear algorithms, including PCA, t-SNE, and UMAP.

Methodologies

PCA: A linear technique that identifies the "Principal Components" (super-metrics) that capture the maximum variance in the data.
t-SNE & UMAP: Advanced non-linear techniques that excel at preserving local clusters, allowing researchers to see how counties with similar multi-metric profiles naturally group together, even when those relationships are highly complex.

Analytic Views:
Interactive Projections Explore the "latent space" of American public health. Counties are plotted as individual points; their proximity on the map represents their statistical similarity across all selected socioeconomic and health factors.
PCA Biplot & Scree Specifically for PCA, these allow you to see both the "Principal Components" themselves and the exact contribution vectors for every raw variable, revealing what each axis "means" in real-world terms.
  • • Regional Discovery: Use the interactive map and chart selection to see how state-level policies or geographic clusters emerge from complex health data.
  • • Hyperparameter Tuning: Manually adjust Perplexity (t-SNE) or Neighbors (UMAP) to refine how local vs. global structures are prioritized in the visual representation.

Predictive Analysis

Intelligent Forecasting & Machine Learning

The Predictive Analysis page leverages advanced Machine Learning algorithms to build robust forecasting models that predict health outcomes based on socioeconomic and environmental factors.

Methodology

We employ a variety of state-of-the-art models including Random Forest, XGBoost, and Neural Networks. The system automatically handles data preprocessing, feature scaling, and model evaluation using nested cross-validation to ensure the most accurate and reliable results.

Key Features:
Model Comparison Compare performance across multiple algorithms (Regression vs. Classification) using metrics like R-squared, RMSE, Accuracy, and F1-score.
Feature Importance Visualize which socioeconomic variables are the strongest predictors for specific health outcomes using interactive Sunburst charts and importance rankings.
  • • Interactive Pipelines: Visualize the entire data science workflow, from data ingestion to final prediction.
  • • In-Browser Computation: All ML models run locally in your browser using Web Workers, ensuring data privacy and immediate feedback.
