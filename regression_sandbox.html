Statistical Methodology

The Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression estimates the relationship between a dependent variable and one or more independent predictors by minimizing the sum of the squares of the differences between the observed and predicted values.

Y = β0 + β1X1 + β2X2 + ... + βkXk + ε

Ridge Regularization (Tikhonov)

When "Ridge" is enabled, the model adds a penalty term proportional to the square of the magnitude of coefficients. This addresses multicollinearity (redundant variables) and prevents overfitting by shrinking coefficients toward zero.

β = (XTX + λI)-1 XTY
  • λ (Lambda): Higher values increase the penalty, leading to smaller coefficients and a "simpler" model.
  • Effect: Reduces variance at the cost of slight bias, stabilizing estimates when variables are highly correlated.

Model Evaluation

  • R-Squared: The proportion of the variance in the dependent variable that is predictable from the independent variable(s).
  • p-value (Pr(>|t|)): The probability of finding the observed, or more extreme, results when the null hypothesis (that the coefficient is 0) is true.
  • Coefficients: The estimated change in the dependent variable for every unit increase in the predictor, holding other variables constant.

Technical Notes

  • Computations are handled entirely on the client-side via the Math.js matrix algebra and jStat probability libraries.
  • Standard errors and p-values are derived from the variance-covariance matrix (XTX)-1 σ2.
  • Missing or null records for any selected variable drop that record entirely (Listwise Deletion).

Understanding Your Model Summary

A plain-English guide to every metric in the output table

R²
R-Squared (Coefficient of Determination)

Measures the proportion of variance in the outcome (Y) that is explained by your predictors. Ranges from 0 to 1.

Example: R² = 0.72 means your predictors explain 72% of the county-level variation in the outcome. The remaining 28% is explained by factors not in your model.
⚠️ A high R² does not guarantee a good model. More predictors always increase R² — use Adjusted R² to account for this.
Adj. R²
Adjusted R-Squared

A version of R² that is penalised for the number of predictors. It goes down if a new predictor adds no real explanatory power.

Rule of thumb: If Adj. R² ≈ R², your predictors are all contributing meaningfully. If Adj. R² is much lower than R², you likely have redundant or noise predictors.
✅ Prefer Adjusted R² when comparing models with different numbers of predictors.
F
F-Statistic

Tests whether the model as a whole explains a statistically significant proportion of the variance — i.e., whether any predictor is useful at all.

Formula: F = (R² / k) / ((1 − R²) / (N − k − 1)), where k = number of predictors, N = observations.
📌 A large F paired with a small Model p-value tells you the model is collectively significant even before looking at individual predictors.
p
Model p-value (Overall F-test)

The probability of observing an F-statistic this large (or larger) if none of the predictors actually mattered.

Interpretation: p < 0.05 → reject the null hypothesis that all coefficients are zero. Your model has statistically significant predictive power.
✅ Always check this first. If Model p-value > 0.05, the model is not statistically significant overall.
N
Observations (N)

The number of complete county records used to fit the model. Records are dropped if any selected variable has a missing value (listwise deletion).

Why it matters: Larger N → more statistical power → smaller standard errors → easier to detect real effects. Fewer than 30 observations can make OLS unreliable.
β
Estimate (Coefficient)

The estimated change in Y for a one-unit increase in this predictor, holding all other predictors constant.

Example: Coefficient for Poverty Rate = 0.42 means that, on average, each additional 1-percentage-point increase in poverty is associated with a 0.42 pp increase in the outcome, after controlling for other variables.
📌 The intercept (β₀) is the predicted value of Y when all predictors equal zero — it rarely has a direct policy interpretation.
SE
Standard Error of the Estimate

Quantifies the uncertainty in the coefficient estimate. A smaller SE relative to the estimate means a more precise estimate.

Confidence Interval: An approximate 95% CI for the coefficient is Estimate ± 1.96 × SE. If this interval does not cross zero, the predictor is significant at p < 0.05.
⚠️ Large SE relative to the Estimate (SE > |Estimate| / 2) suggests the predictor coefficient is imprecisely estimated — possibly due to multicollinearity.
t
t-value (t-statistic)

The ratio of the coefficient to its standard error: t = Estimate / SE. Tests whether the coefficient is significantly different from zero.

Rule of thumb: |t| > 2 generally indicates statistical significance at the 5% level (for large N). The farther t is from zero, the stronger the evidence against H₀: β = 0.
p
Pr(>|t|) — Variable p-value

The probability of observing a t-statistic this extreme if the true coefficient were zero. Derived from the t-distribution with (N − k − 1) degrees of freedom, computed via jStat.

Decision: p < 0.05 → the predictor is a statistically significant contributor to the model at the 5% level.
✅ Computed via the two-tailed t-distribution CDF using the jStat library.

Significance stars are a compact shorthand for the p-value of each predictor's t-test. They follow the standard R-style convention:

Symbol p-value range Interpretation
*** p < 0.001 Extremely significant — very strong evidence against H₀
** 0.001 ≤ p < 0.01 Highly significant — strong evidence
* 0.01 ≤ p < 0.05 Significant at the conventional 5% level
. 0.05 ≤ p < 0.1 Marginally significant — weak evidence, interpret with caution
(blank) p ≥ 0.1 Not significant — insufficient evidence to reject H₀
⚠️ Important caveat: Statistical significance ≠ practical significance. A coefficient can be highly significant (p < 0.001) but tiny in magnitude, or non-significant but large in effect, due to low power (small N). Always consider both p-values and effect sizes together.

Here is a step-by-step guide to interpreting a full regression output from this sandbox:

1
Check Model p-value first. If Model p > 0.05, stop — the model as a whole is not significant and individual coefficients are unreliable.
2
Read R² and Adjusted R². These tell you how well your predictors collectively explain the outcome. An Adj. R² of 0.60+ is considered strong for public-health cross-sectional data.
3
Scan the Sig. column. Predictors with *** or ** are the most reliable contributors. Those with no stars may be noise.
4
Read Estimates (coefficients) for direction & magnitude. A positive coefficient means the predictor and outcome move together; negative means they move in opposite directions.
5
Check Standard Errors relative to Estimates. If SE is close to or larger than |Estimate|, the effect is poorly estimated — consider removing correlated predictors.
6
Inspect the Residuals vs Fitted plot. A random scatter (no pattern) confirms OLS assumptions are roughly met. Obvious curves suggest a non-linear relationship or omitted predictors.
📚 Libraries used: Coefficients and standard errors are computed via Math.js matrix algebra ((XᵀX)⁻¹Xᵀy). p-values use the two-tailed t-distribution from jStat. The F-statistic p-value uses the F-distribution from jStat.
