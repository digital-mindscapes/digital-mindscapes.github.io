Spatial Autocorrelation Guide

What is Spatial Autocorrelation?

Spatial autocorrelation measures the degree to which a variable is geographically dependent. It asks: "Are values at one location similar to values at nearby locations?"

Clustered (Positive) Similar values group together. Hotspots near hotspots, coldspots near coldspots.
Dispersed (Negative) Dissimilar values are neighbors (checkerboard pattern).

Global Moran's I Statistic

The Global Moran's I quantifies the overall spatial autocorrelation for the study region. It measures how similar each county is to its neighbors and averages those assessments across the map. The statistic is formally defined as:

$$I = \frac{n \sum_i \sum_j w_{ij}(Y_i - \bar Y)(Y_j - \bar Y)} {(\sum_{i \neq j} w_{ij}) \sum_i (Y_i - \bar Y)^2}$$
Global Moran's I Equation

Where:

  • $n$: Number of counties.
  • $Y_i$: Value of the variable in county $i$.
  • $\bar Y$: Mean of the variable across all counties.
  • $w_{ij}$: Spatial weight between county $i$ and $j$.

Interpretation:

  • I > E[I]: Positive spatial autocorrelation (Clustering).
  • I < E[I]: Negative spatial autocorrelation (Dispersion).
  • I ≈ E[I]: Random distribution. ($E[I] = -1/(n-1)$)

The Z-Score and P-Value are obtained via Monte Carlo randomization (permutations) to determine if the observed pattern is statistically significant.

Local Indicators of Spatial Association (LISA)

To identify specific local clusters or outliers, we calculate the Local Moran's I for each individual region ($i$):

$$I_i = \frac{n (Y_i - \bar Y)}{\sum_j (Y_j - \bar Y)^2} \sum_j w_{ij}(Y_j - \bar Y)$$
Local Moran's I (LISA) Equation

LISA allows us to decompose Global Moran's I into the contribution of each location, visualizing specifically where significant "Hotspots" or "Outliers" exist.

Multiple Testing & Significance

When performing LISA analysis on thousands of counties, the probability of finding significant clusters by pure chance increases (the "Multiple Comparisons Problem"). To ensure robustness, we provide two correction methods:

  • Bonferroni Correction: A very conservative adjustment that controls the family-wise error rate.
    $$\alpha_{Bonferroni} = \frac{\alpha}{n}$$
  • Šidák Correction: A slightly less conservative adjustment.
    $$\alpha_{\check{S}id\acute{a}k} = 1 - (1 - \alpha)^{1/n}$$
  • False Discovery Rate (FDR): The Benjamini-Hochberg procedure, which controls the expected proportion of false discoveries. It is generally less conservative than Bonferroni and highly recommended for large spatial datasets.
    $$P_{(k)} \le \frac{k}{n} \alpha$$

High permutation counts (e.g., 99,999) are recommended when using these corrections to ensure the p-value precision is sufficient to meet the lowered significance thresholds.

Spatial Clusters and Outliers (LISA)

Local Moran's I identifies two types of spatial association:

HH: High-High
LL: Low-Low
Spatial Clusters (Cores) Regions with similar values grouped together. HH are "Hotspots" (high units near high units), and LL are "Coldspots". Strictly speaking, these are the cores of clusters.
HL: High-Low
LH: Low-High
Spatial Outliers Individual units that differ significantly from their neighbors. A HL outlier has a high value in a low-value neighborhood, while LH is the inverse.

Note: The "Significance Map" shows the stability of these patterns via pseudo p-values. Darker green indicates higher statistical confidence (less likely to occur under spatial randomness).

Neighborhood Methods

To determine spatial dependency, you can choose between three methods:

  • Queen Contiguity (Adjacency): Neighbors are those that share a boundary or vertex (approximated by overlapping geographical extents).
  • K-Nearest Neighbors (K=8): Identifies the 8 closest geographical units based on their centroids, ensuring every unit has a fixed number of neighbors.
  • Distance-Based: Identifies all neighbors within a fixed geographic radius (approx. 100 miles), which can lead to varying numbers of neighbors per unit.
